Narrative Systems · Techland (2022-2024)

Dying Light 2 Redux

Reimagining an entire AAA narrative without changing the gameplay.

The Challenge

Great Gameplay, Broken Story

Techland had a problem. Their AAA open-world game Dying Light 2 was a hit — players loved the gameplay. But the story had bombed. Reviewers and players complained of a plot that made it impossible to invest emotionally, branching that didn't honor player choices, and no coherent reason to finish most missions beyond "follow the yellow marker."

I was brought in as a consultant to assess the damage. My conclusion: the narrative wasn't salvageable. But the game itself was incredibly fun. It deserved a story worthy of the gameplay.

I was hired as Narrative Director to lead the charge, building a team of writers to rip apart a living game and put it back together again.

"The game itself was incredibly fun. It deserved a story worthy of the gameplay."

The Approach

The Tetris Experiment at Scale

The game designer Clint Hocking once proposed that by changing only the narrative skin of Tetris — telling players, for example, that the blocks were families being packed into cattle cars, and when filled, would be sent off to camps — you could give the game an entirely new meaning without touching the gameplay.

This was my chance to test that theory at massive scale.

The work required a ground-up rebuild of the narrative layer — the connective tissue between the button presses:

New character backstory that aligned player and protagonist motivation

Restructured missions so each answer became the question leading to the next

Real branching with compounding consequences — choices that mattered

A new ending built on callbacks to decisions made throughout

As Narrative Director, I was the vision holder for the entire initiative — hiring and leading a writers' room, overseeing all creative departments, working hand-in-hand with design and animation. Together we produced over a thousand pages of ambitious, intricately branching narrative architecture, all designed to wrap around existing gameplay like a new skin.

What I Learned

Meaning Without Mechanics

The missions stayed the same. The mechanics stayed the same. But by rewriting the context — why you were doing each thing, who you were doing it for, what you stood to lose — the experience transformed.

"You can change what something means without changing what it does."

A language model's capabilities are its mechanics. But what makes it feel trustworthy, engaging, worth returning to — that's the narrative layer. The personality. The motivation. The emotional coherence.

Techland taught me that even when you can't change an underlying system, you can change everything about what that system makes you feel.
