World Building · Another Axiom (2024-2025)

Orion Drift

How do you tell story in a game that's not about story?

The Challenge

Story Without Story

I was brought in to lead world creation on a major initiative from a game studio at the top of their game — Another Axiom, creators of "Gorilla Tag," the first $100 million VR game.

They had created an amazingly playable social game built around being robots in outer space. But they had a question they couldn't answer.

How do you tell story in a game that's not about story?

The Approach

The Storyworld as Container

My insight: Even in a social game that's about being with your friends, players want to feel the storyworld all around them. They want to be immersed together.

So, I borrowed a concept from amusement park design. Guests engage at different levels:

  • Waders are happy just to be there, riding the ride.
  • Swimmers want to explore every nook and cranny.
  • And Mermaids never want to leave.

For Orion Drift, I designed for all three simultaneously:

For Waders

Every sign and poster carried a fragment of world-building — there if you notice, invisible if you don't.

For Swimmers

Hidden rooms with environmental storytelling — secret spaces that reward curiosity and gameplay with friends.

And for Mermaids

An entire history of the future, delivered as an interactive ARG through the game's Discord server.

What I Learned

Container, Not Content

"Cinderella's castle at Disneyland doesn't tell you a story. It makes you feel like you're in one."

In experiential storytelling, the storyworld is the container, not the content. It creates belonging and sparks curiosity — then lets players fill it with their own meaning.

A great AI companion isn't there to tell you a story. But it must exist inside a storyworld — with a history, a personality, a reason for being. That's what makes it feel like someone, not something.
