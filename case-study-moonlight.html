AI + Social VR · Meta / Jon Snoddy (2025)

Murder at Moonlight Lodge

Designing a social game where conversation with AI characters is the core mechanic.

The Challenge

From Demo to Experience

Jon Snoddy — former head of R&D at Disney Imagineering — had put together a stellar team and built an AI character prototype that got Meta excited. The demo was impressive: characters that could converse, respond and remember with multiple players at once.

But a demo isn't a game. The team had no one who could turn this technology into something people would actually want to play — especially in social VR, where the experience needed to work for groups of friends.

"How do you design a social game where the core mechanic is conversation with AI characters?"

The Approach

Conversation as Gameplay

I designed a multiplayer social mystery game — think Clue meets Among Us in a haunted mansion. Players work together to solve a murder, with AI characters who know more than they're willing to say.

The principles I developed made a very fun game, but were also deeply instructive about designing for AI:

Characters as lock-and-key puzzles

Every AI character has something they're hiding and multiple ways to coax it out. The player's job is to figure out how to unlock them — emotionally, socially, or strategically.

"Yes and" the player

Multiple paths honor player agency. Characters always have more than one method to unlock their clues. The game never punishes a choice; it meets players where they are. This is what makes interaction feel like collaboration.

Memory creates relationship

Characters remember not just what you said, but how you made them feel. Kindness opens doors in future sessions. Cruelty closes them. Emotional continuity is what makes an AI feel like someone you have history with.

Depth is revealed over time

Characters have layers that only unlock after your 5th, 10th, 20th conversation across multiple sessions. The relationship rewards returning — there's always more to discover.

Social dynamics layer on top of AI interactions

The AI characters aren't the whole game; they're the substrate on which human social dynamics play out (trading clues, lying, alliances, betrayal). When characters and players are interacting as a group, distinctions between reality and fiction disappear.

What I Learned

Design Over Technology

The project was a deep dive into AI character technology and prompt engineering — what works, what doesn't, and why.

Open-ended conversation is the trap so many AI experiences fall into. Open-ended conversation is boring. You can talk about anything, so there's no reason to talk about anything in particular.

The solution isn't better AI. It's better design: gamifying conversations, leading with mystery, giving users goals to pursue and multiple strategies to achieve them.

This is where my background became essential. I drew on principles from both game design (player agency within structured possibility spaces) and experiential theater (scenes with clear dramatic stakes guided by classic improv structure of intentions and events) to create interactions that felt genuinely playful.

The hardest problem in AI interaction isn't the technology. It's the experience design.
