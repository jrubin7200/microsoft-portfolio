A Life of Exploring. A Life Explored.

To make an AI that feels truly human, you need someone who has spent their life asking what it means to be human.

Someone who has spent their life exploring and playing and asking questions and reading the great philosophers and mystics and trying to capture life through art — while at the same time, working deeply enough with technology to push at the boundaries of what art can do for humanity.

"To make an AI that feels truly human, you need someone who has spent their life asking what it means to be human."

So, let me tell you a little bit about my life outside my LinkedIn profile.

I was born in Manhattan, raised on a Buddhist ashram in the cornfields of the Midwest, and survived high school in Hollywood.

I dropped out of college to travel the world for years, to find my voice as a writer.

I fell in love in Paris while living in a bookstore, and discovered the depths of the human heart.

I biked across Europe, hiked across the French Alps, and the Himalayas in Nepal, and discovered what the human body could be pushed to.

I spent half a year in the British library in Florence, trying to read everything that had ever been written so far, from the Romans and Shakespeare to Proust and Ulysses.

I spent a winter in a cabin with only the mystics for company — Rumi, Meister Eckhart, Nisargadatta — trying to understand the truths that connect us all.

"I fell in love in Paris while living in a bookstore, and discovered the depths of the human heart."

My primary skill set has always been as a storyteller — but focused from the beginning with curiosity about pushing the frontiers of what narrative was capable of.

At age 12, I won the statewide writing contest in Illinois for a short book that merged characters from a dozen different genres. This is when I knew writing was for me.

I graduated college with a novel of historical fiction, about the French poet Baudelaire — but it constantly broke the fourth wall to ask: how much can we ever really know about another person?

My first screenplays challenged the conventions of what a screenplay could be, delving into both heartbreaking European romance, inspired by filmmakers like Krystof Kieslowski, Eric Rohmer, Arnaud Desplechin — and deep sci-fi inspired by Kurt Vonnegut, Neal Stephenson and Philip K Dick.

One of my first screenplays won the Nicholl Fellowship, the most prestigious writing award for unproduced screenplays in Hollywood, and paved the way for a decade of work in film and TV. But I was constantly being told my vision was un-producible, too out there for what could fit on a silver screen.

It was in the interactive world of video games that I finally found the right place for my imagination.

On one hand, I became known for bringing deeply human characters to big AAA games, like the iconic Ezio Auditore in Assassin's Creed 2 — a game that won numerous writing awards and is often listed as the best of the series. At the same time, in game design I found the world of player agency in a possibility space, the explorable story world. I helped build massive narrative systems in blockbuster AAA games like Destiny.

I was early into Virtual Reality, thrilled at the opportunity to invent an entirely new medium of storytelling, winning awards for my work and leading largescale projects with major IP.

And I came to specialize in creating branching narratives — from learning under the tutelage of the best at Telltale Games, to winning an Interactive Emmy for my work at Netflix, to leading the creation of a profoundly ambitious 1,000 pages of byzantine narrative architecture at Techland.

Throughout, I've always been trying to shape systems and storyworlds that were deeply immersive, that gave you the feeling of true Story Living — not just watching a story, living inside one. But even when designing thousand-page-narratives, I always bumped against the limits of what pre-authored content could do.

The player can only ever choose between paths that you designed for them to take.

"Not watching a story — living inside one."

When the current wave of AI arrived, I recognized it immediately as the technology that could finally enable what I've been imagining: stories that truly respond, characters that truly listen, worlds that truly adapt.

The technology is capable of knowing us each in profoundly personal ways. The best AI of the future will be capable of inviting each of us into storyworlds that feel like they were designed just for us, bringing us the exact challenges, and exactly the catharsis, that we need to grow as people.

"Stories that truly respond, characters that truly listen, worlds that truly adapt."

But that level of personal data about us is incredibly dangerous if misused — the same system can manipulate you, addict you, surveil you. If we don't guide these AI co-creators with intention, we'll get hollow simulacra and endless "dystopian AI slop."

The technology is coming fast. The question is how we shape it — as capitalists and technologists, or as humanists and storytellers?

My childhood (from Ashram to Hollywood), my years reading the great philosophers and mystics, the life spent writing deeply human characters and inventing narrative systems — it wasn't wandering. It was preparation.

Now, the most exciting challenge in the world is to help an AI feel human. That's not a technical problem. It's an artistic one.

The Core Philosophy

Agency vs. Catharsis

Over the course of my career, I've learned that interactive storytelling has a central tension at its heart.

Agency

What players crave — the freedom to do what you want, to shape the world, to matter.

Catharsis

What audiences crave — structure, meaning, the emotional payoff of a well-told story.

These two forces are fundamentally at odds. The more freedom you give, the harder it is to deliver a meaningful arc. The tighter the story, the less the player feels in control.

"The magic will always be in the balance — a companion that both meets you where you are, and takes you somewhere meaningful."

Most creators live on one side or the other. Writers craft beautiful stories but struggle with systems. Designers build elegant mechanics but can't land an emotional beat.

I've spent my career learning to hold both.

I write deeply human characters. I also architect thousand-page branching systems.

I understand story structure and possibility spaces.

I know how to make you cry, and how to make you feel like it was your choices that led to that moment.

For AI companions, this duality is everything. An AI that only responds (pure agency) feels empty. An AI that only performs (pure catharsis) feels scripted. The magic will always be in the balance — a companion that both meets you where you are, and takes you somewhere meaningful.

On the future of interactive storytelling and radical agency

Toward Humanist AI

Last week I spoke to a hundred freshmen at an emerging media arts program in the Midwest — the future builders. I asked how many were using AI in their creative work.

One hand went up.

These are students at an emerging media arts program! And they're not touching the most transformative creative technology of their generation.

Why? Because they're terrified of it.

They don't see the magic. They see the monster coming to destroy their futures before those futures even begin.

Mustafa Suleyman talks about "the disconnect between people who develop technologies and the communities who use them." I saw that disconnect in a room full of eighteen-year-olds who should be the most excited people on the planet about these tools.

"They don't see the magic. They see the monster coming to destroy their futures before those futures even begin."

If the next generation sees AI as an existential threat rather than a trusted companion, that technology doesn't have a long future. If we can't address their fears, protect their livelihoods, and earn their trust — it doesn't matter that we're building the most powerful tools in human history.

The technology is coming fast. If it's going to earn human trust, it needs to be shaped by people who have spent their life exploring, and exploring what it means to be human.

Thank you.

— Joshua Rubin
